# Rabbit Hole

## Product Vision

### One Sentence

A visual map of everything you read and how ideas connect.

---

## Why

Most reading tools treat articles as isolated items in a list:

* bookmarks
* read-it-later queues
* folders
* tags

This matches storage but not thinking.

People often remember knowledge relationally:

> I found this article through that one.
>
> This post challenged that idea.
>
> These three articles explain the same concept from different angles.
>
> I spent months circling around this topic without realizing it.

The connections are often more valuable than the articles themselves.

Yet these connections are usually lost.

---

## Vision

Rabbit Hole turns reading history into an evolving knowledge map.

Every article becomes a place.

Every relationship becomes a path.

Over time, users build a landscape of ideas rather than a collection of saved links.

The product acts as an external memory for curiosity and intellectual exploration.

---

## Core Belief

Knowledge is not a tree.

Knowledge is a graph.

Ideas have multiple contexts, multiple influences, and multiple relationships.

Understanding emerges from seeing these relationships together.

The product should help users answer questions such as:

* How did I arrive at this idea?
* Which topics keep reappearing?
* Which articles changed my thinking?
* Which sources influence one another?
* What areas of knowledge am I exploring?
* Where are the gaps?

---

## Product Principles

### Capture Must Be Effortless

The moment of curiosity is fleeting.

Capturing something should be nearly frictionless.

The user should never have to decide:

* where to put something
* which folder it belongs in
* whether it is important enough

The system should preserve the opportunity to think later.

---

### The Graph Is The Product

The graph is not a visualization of stored notes.

The graph is the primary interface.

Position, proximity, and relationships all carry meaning.

Users should feel like they are navigating a map rather than managing files.

---

### Relationships Are First-Class

An article rarely matters in isolation.

What matters is:

* what it references
* what references it
* what ideas it resembles
* what questions it answers
* what other paths it opens

The product should make discovering and traversing these relationships feel natural.

---

### Spatial Memory Matters

Humans remember location and shape surprisingly well.

People remember:

> It was near the distributed systems cluster.

> I put that article between databases and programming languages.

> This idea sat on the edge of another topic.

The product should leverage spatial memory instead of forcing knowledge into lists and hierarchies.

---

### Maps Should Evolve

A graph should grow organically.

Clusters emerge.

Topics drift.

Previously unrelated areas become connected.

The product should allow users to witness the evolution of their own thinking.

---

## What Success Looks Like

After months of use, users no longer see:

"A collection of saved articles."

Instead they see:

"A map of my intellectual life."

They can point to regions of the graph and say:

* This is where I learned distributed systems.
* This cluster changed how I think about software architecture.
* This path led me from one article to an entirely new field.

The product becomes less like a bookmarking tool and more like a personal atlas of ideas.
